Research

Field experiments, continuous flux monitoring, and process-based modeling across rice and corn systems — improving soil health and water management while mitigating greenhouse-gas emissions.

USDA-NRCS · 2023–2027 · Belzoni & Isola, MS

Greenhouse gas emissions in tailwater-reuse row rice

As part of the USDA-NRCS project “How low can you go? Automating tailwater reuse in rice,” the lab quantifies greenhouse-gas emissions and global warming potential in continuously flooded versus end-blocked, tailwater-recirculating furrow-irrigated rice.

Using static flux chambers and six eddy covariance towers across paired farm sites near Belzoni and Isola, the team measures methane, nitrous oxide, and carbon dioxide, relating emissions to grain yield and irrigation practice and feeding a rice sub-model for USDA COMET-Farm.

◆ Key findings

  • Recirculating tailwater in furrow-irrigated rice reduced methane (CH₄) emissions — without a yield penalty.
  • Seasonal CH₄ was ~76% lower in the furrow-irrigated upper position than in continuously flooded rice, while the lower position ran higher: position within the field matters.
  • Productivity–emission tradeoffs can leave yield-scaled emissions comparable across irrigation systems.
  • One season of static-chamber data (2024) is complete; six flux towers now measure CH₄ and evapotranspiration, with off-season processing producing seasonal CH₄ and ET budgets.

MSU (WRRI) · 2025–present · eddy covariance

Evapotranspiration and ecosystem exchange in row rice

This work evaluates how water-saving irrigation — furrow irrigation and tailwater recirculation (alternate wetting and drying) — affects evapotranspiration, water-use efficiency, and ecosystem carbon and water exchange in row rice.

Six eddy covariance towers at farmer fields measure carbon dioxide, water vapor, and energy fluxes from planting to harvest, combined with satellite remote sensing and machine learning to quantify the ecological footprint of U.S. rice.

◆ Key findings

  • Remotely sensed ET alone correlated only weakly with eddy-covariance ET in rice.
  • ET was strongly driven by meteorology; weather variables explained most of the variation, with remote sensing adding a correction.
  • A random-forest model combining tower and satellite data predicted ET with R² ≈ 0.46 (RMSE 1.18 mm/day); temperature, solar radiation, and humidity were the top predictors.
  • Additional higher-resolution ground data are expected to improve accuracy.

MSU (WRRI) · 2024–present · Brooksville, MS

Improved nitrogen management to mitigate greenhouse gases in corn

This trial tests whether nitrogen strategies that maximize nitrogen recovery in crop biomass can minimize direct nitrous-oxide emissions in continuous corn.

Field experiments evaluate nitrogen rate, application depth, surface-trench management, and a urease inhibitor (NBPT), with weekly to biweekly fluxes measured by trace-gas analyzers and smart chambers to identify protected-fertility strategies.

◆ Key findings

  • Higher nitrogen rates increased CO₂-equivalent and yield-scaled emissions.
  • Contrary to expectation, closing fertilizer trenches at the surface tended to increase nitrous-oxide (N₂O) emissions.
  • Nitrogen stabilizers showed no measurable reduction in total emissions in rainfed corn.

MSU (WRRI) · 2024–present · Blackbelt region, MS

Cover cropping and no-tillage in corn

This project quantifies how conservation practices — no-till and cover cropping — reduce greenhouse-gas emissions and build climate-smart corn systems for Mississippi's Blackbelt.

A plot-scale split-plot trial at Brooksville pairs weekly flux measurements with deep soil sampling to 1 m; field data drive DAYCENT simulations of carbon and nitrogen transformations and global warming potential.

Mississippi Corn Promotion Board · Brooksville, MS

Rapid on-farm assessment of soil nitrate in corn

This project identifies farmer-friendly methods for soil nitrate (NO3−) testing and evaluates whether low-cost colorimetric kits can serve as a rapid tool for monitoring soil water quality in corn.

Across field campaigns at Brooksville, three soil-water sampling techniques — suction-cup lysimeters, drainage catch basins, and soil-slurry filtrate — are compared for effort and cost, and colorimetric strips and cadmium-reduction kits are benchmarked against laboratory LC-MS, with strip color read by handheld colorimeter and ImageJ to build calibration curves.

ICL Growing Solutions · 2025–2026

Polysulfate amendments to reduce methane from rice

This study tests whether polysulfate-based soil amendments reduce methane production in flooded rice by limiting the acetate available to methane-producing bacteria.

Across three Delta farmer fields, treated and control plots are compared; soils are analyzed for acetate and incubated to measure methane bursts by GC-MS at MSU's Hand Laboratories.

MSU (WRRI) · 2024–present · Brooksville, MS

Organic crop production initiative

The lab is transitioning a 10-acre research plot to USDA organic certification to study regenerative practices in row crops. Current trials evaluate cover-crop termination timing and methods for organic corn–soybean rotations in a humid environment, generating data to seed larger regional proposals.

USDA-ARS Cooperative Agreement · 2024–2029

Climate-smart agriculture with remote sensing, ML & big data

This cooperative agreement identifies agronomic interventions for climate-smart agriculture by integrating remote sensing, cloud computing, machine learning, and big-data approaches with field agronomy and hydrologic modeling across Mississippi row-crop systems.
