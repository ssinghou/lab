People

A multidisciplinary group spanning soil science, agronomy, engineering, and environmental monitoring.

Principal Investigator

Dr. Madhav Dhakal

Assistant Research Professor · Lab Director

Dr. Dhakal leads research on soil-plant-water relations, evapotranspiration, greenhouse gas emissions, soil health, and climate-smart agriculture. He earned his Ph.D. in Plant and Soil Science from Texas Tech University and has authored more than 20 peer-reviewed publications.

Postdoctoral Researchers

Dr. Kharla Mendez

Postdoctoral Associate

Dr. Kharla Mendez is a Postdoctoral Associate at Mississippi State University, where her research investigates the impacts of water-management systems on crop yield and trace-gas fluxes. She trains and supervises laboratory and field personnel, manages sampling workflows, supports instrument operation, and troubleshoots across the lab's research projects.

She earned her Ph.D. in Molecular Biosciences from Arkansas State University in 2024, with dissertation research on how high night-time temperature stress affects carbohydrate partitioning and agronomic parameters of rice; that work included developing a high-throughput Fourier-transform infrared (FTIR) technique to quantify non-structural carbohydrates in rice flag leaves. Her expertise spans analytical chemistry, soil and crop science, environmental monitoring, method development, and field phenotyping. She is based in Jonesboro, Arkansas.

Email: ksm485@msstate.edu

Dr. Amrit Shrestha

Postdoctoral Research Associate

Dr. Amrit Shrestha is a Postdoctoral Research Associate at Mississippi State University and an agricultural engineer specializing in UAV remote sensing, geospatial analytics, and data-driven crop modeling. He develops methods for crop phenology monitoring, management-zone delineation, and machine-learning–based soybean yield prediction using satellite and uncrewed aerial systems (UAS) imagery.

He earned his Ph.D. in Agricultural and Biological Engineering from Mississippi State University, an M.S. in Agricultural Engineering from King Mongkut's Institute of Technology Ladkrabang, Thailand, and a B.S. in Mechanical Engineering from Kathmandu University, Nepal. His expertise spans remote sensing, hyperspectral sensing, neural networks, UAV-based phenotyping, and near-infrared spectroscopy, and he has built high-accuracy models for predicting cotton, corn, and soybean yields as well as plant height.

Email: as5085@msstate.edu

Graduate Students

Pankaj Prashad Joshi

Graduate Research Assistant

Pankaj Prashad Joshi is a graduate research assistant at the Mississippi Water Resources Research Institute, under the supervision of Dr. Madhav Dhakal. He earned his bachelor's degree in agriculture from Tribhuvan University, Nepal (2014) and his master's in Agronomy from Agriculture and Forestry University, Nepal (2016). He is pursuing his Ph.D. in Agronomy in the Department of Plant and Soil Sciences at Mississippi State University.

His research interests include climate-smart agriculture, greenhouse gas emissions, and sustainable agriculture, with experience in conservation agriculture practices, varietal improvement, and fertilizer management in row crops.

He is currently investigating the effect of protected fertilizer and regenerative management on greenhouse gas emissions from corn using trace-gas analyzers, and aims to build a validated model for estimating gas flux in response to management practices.

Oluwadamilare Oloyede

Graduate Research Assistant

Oluwadamilare Oloyede is a graduate research assistant in the Mississippi Water Resources Research Institute. He holds a bachelor's degree in agronomy (University of Ibadan, Nigeria), a master's in soil resources management (University of Ibadan, Nigeria), and an international joint master's in soils and global change with a specialization in physical land resources (Ghent University, Belgium & Aarhus University, Denmark).

His research interests are in soil-plant-atmosphere relationships — how weather and climate affect crop production, and how crops respond to climate-related stresses. He develops strategies for efficient resource use by crops, spanning soil water management, soil nutrient evaluation, and soil physics.

He is pursuing a Ph.D. in Agronomy at Mississippi State University, focusing on the impact of alternate wetting and drying and tailwater recirculation on greenhouse gas emissions and evapotranspiration in rice, combining eddy covariance data with remote sensing to quantify the ecological footprint of U.S. rice production.
